# Yeo-Language (十の字)

**Yeo-Language (Nosa, Tonoji, Japanese: 十の字)** is an auxiliary constructed language created by Nianago06 with the concept of being **“the simplest language”**, featuring the smallest number of root words.
Since this language is the sole creation of Nianago06, it is an unfinished language with insufficient academic documentation, details on the creation process, proof of linguistic consistency, and verification of flaws.

This language was created strictly as an auxiliary language, not as a native language or a perfect language. Therefore, gestures and proper nouns from foreign languages may be used to supplement linguistic information.

To sustain the language with its limited number of roots, Yeo-Language is centered around a fictional worldview and religious beliefs. However, these are not directly discussed within the language itself; they are used solely as the foundation for word formation and as the meanings of the roots.

---

# Features

The most notable feature of Yeo-Language is that **its fundamental vocabulary (root lexemes) is limited to only ten items**.
This design originated from the creator’s question regarding Toki Pona—the world’s most minimal constructed language—specifically why “lizard” and “insect” exist as separate words.

In Japanese and Classical Chinese, the character **蟲** historically unified such creatures under a single concept.
This insight led to the initiation of the Yeo-Language project.

The language is created with the following purposes:
- organizing thought
- simple communication
- cross-cultural understanding

assisting individuals who have difficulty acquiring language

# Name

**十の字 (Tō-no-ji)** — derived from the number of root words (ten).

**Yeo-Language** — yeo means “person/human,” similar to naming conventions in Ainu or Inuktitut.

**Nosa** — from Noosa, meaning “one’s own language.”

# Writing System

Although Yeo-Language has its own script, it is primarily written using the **Latin alphabet**.

To avoid confusion, **affixes attached to words other than *wa*, *no*, *o*, and *sa* are connected with a hyphen (-)**.

## Examples

| Form | Meaning |
|-----|-------------|
| Tuye | bird / to fly |
| o-Tuye | a bird |
| sa-Tuye | to fly |
| o-sa-Tuye | flight |
| Tuye-o | something that used to be a bird |

### Writing rules:

- default: lowercase
- capitalize only the first independent word of a sentence
- prefixes/articles at sentence start remain lowercase

# Phonology
Yeo-Language contains 6 consonants and 5 vowels, plus 3 long vowels.

### Consonants

/ p, w, t, n, s, j /

### Vowels

/ a, e, i, o, u, aa, oo, ooo /

The language uses a **pitch accent system**, where accent placement indicates word boundaries.

# Grammar

Yeo-Language follows an **SVO (Subject–Verb–Object)** word order.

### Sentence markers

| Marker | Function |
|---|----------|
| o | nominal marker (used after subject in nominal sentences) |
| sa | verbal marker (used after subject in verbal sentences) |
| wa | politeness, topic, conjunction |
| no | semantic supplement, addition, relational marker |

There is **no plural** and no **passive voice**.
Tense exists but is rarely used.

 ## Nouns

- Proper nouns from other languages are used unchanged.
- Parts of speech are determined by grammar, not lexicon.
- o is used for emphasis or nominalization.

### Examples

**O sa o no sa**
 ≒ I grasp and release.

**O sa o no o-sa**
 ≒ I grasp you.

 Short sentences often allow multiple interpretations; translations begin with ≒ or ≂.

 ## Adjectives

- Formed by adding **no** (e.g., ○○no).
- Adjectives are pre-modifying, unlike most other modifiers.
- Common adjectives: Tuno, Tano.

### Example

**Tuno-yeo**
 ≒ a good / strong / big person

 “Above the person” = **Tu no yeo**, distinguished by accent.

 ## Prepositions
 
- Words following no inherit the same part of speech as the preceding word.
- To change part of speech, use **o** or **sa**.

 ## Verbs
 
- Verbs appear as **sa-○○**.
- Nominalized verbs appear as **o-sa-○○**.

## Conjunctions

- Sentence-initial: **Wa + suffix**
- Within a sentence: **No**

## Accent Rules

- The first syllable of any independent word has high pitch.
- The markers o and sa have high pitch.
- The standalone no has high pitch.
- All unspecified positions default to low pitch.
